# About Me

I'm an incoming Ph.D. student in Computer Science at the University of Virginia, advised by [Prof. Ferdinando Fioretto](https://nandofioretto.github.io/) as part of the **Responsible AI for Science and Engineering (RAISE)** group. I'm honored to be fully supported by the UVA Computer Science Scholar Fellowship. I hold a B.Sc. in Mathematics from Beloit College.

My research agenda focuses on a single question: **under what provable guarantees is it safe to deploy generative AI systems of arbitrary capability?** I identify this research program as **Containment** - the discipline of building generative AI that operates within provably bounded regions of behavior, where the constraints define the container and the intelligence operates freely inside it. Containment is a foundational mechanism within AI safety, oriented toward *provable behavioral bounds* rather than learned value alignment, behavioral control, or post-hoc oversight.

The historical analogs are instructive: physics has containment for reactors and plasma, biology has containment for BSL-4 pathogens, cryptography has containment through sandboxing. Each is a mature engineering discipline built around the same insight - *you don't need to limit power, you need to bound where power can act*. My work applies this discipline to cognition.

I'm also interested in collaborative and open-source AI safety research, and I'm always happy to connect with others working on related problems.

## Research Interests

I work on the concept of **AI containment - the process of discovery, certification, composition, and mechanistic enforcement of constraints in generative AI**. I distribute my efforts through the following four research directions:

### 1. Constraint discovery

The first prerequisite of containment is knowing what a model must obey. In well-studied domains this is straightforward - Newton's laws, conservation of mass, regulatory thresholds - but the high-stakes deployment settings that matter most (drug design, climate intervention, autonomous scientific discovery, multi-agent economic systems) involve constraint sets that are *partially known, partially implicit, and partially yet to be discovered*. Hand-specification by domain experts is brittle: it scales poorly, misses tacit constraints that experts know but never articulate, and produces specifications that fail silently when the deployment distribution shifts. Constraint discovery addresses this gap by treating the constraint set itself as a learnable object - extractable from data, from known mechanistic structure, from regulatory text, and from expert demonstration - and then verifiable against ground truth where ground truth exists. The central research questions are: *What is the right hypothesis class for constraints - algebraic, differential, logical, probabilistic, or hybrid?* *What are the sample-complexity and identifiability conditions under which the true constraint set can be recovered?* *And how do we quantify the residual risk from constraints that remain undiscovered?* This thread builds the **input layer** of containment: it determines what the rest of the program is enforcing.

### 2. Probabilistic Certification

The second prerequisite is the ability to *state and verify* that a generative model satisfies a given constraint set with quantified confidence. Current practice in alignment offers behavioral evidence - refusal rates, benchmark scores, red-team outcomes - but no formal guarantees that bound the probability of constraint violation under distributional shift, adversarial input, or novel domains. Classical formal verification offers such guarantees but does not scale to frontier-model regimes. The research opportunity lies in the middle ground: *probabilistic certification at scale*, drawing on PAC-Bayes theory, conformal prediction, interval and abstract interpretation, statistical model checking, and randomized smoothing. The objective is a certification machinery that takes a model and a constraint set as input and returns a *bounded-error guarantee* on constraint satisfaction — a guarantee that is sound, composable across constraints, and tractable for systems with billions of parameters. The central questions are: *What guarantee classes are achievable for what constraint classes at what scale?* *How do we trade off the tightness of bounds against the cost of certification?* *And how do we extend certification from static models to systems that learn, adapt, or interact during deployment?* This thread is the **technical core** of the program: certification with bounded error is what transforms *"alignment by training"* into *"safety by guarantee"*.

### 3. Constraint Composition

The third prerequisite is recognizing that no real deployment of generative AI involves a single stakeholder or a single constraint source. A medical AI must simultaneously respect clinical guidelines, regulatory requirements (FDA, EMA), institutional policies, patient preferences, and ethical principles drawn from multiple traditions. These constraint sources are independently authored, partially overlapping, and often in genuine conflict - and the problem of *composing* them principally has been almost entirely neglected by current safety research. Naive composition (intersection of constraints) yields infeasibility; naive prioritization (lexicographic ordering) hides the political work of choosing the order. The technical position I take is that constraint composition is fundamentally a problem in **social choice theory and mechanism design** applied to formal specifications: it requires conflict detection, principled adjudication, and transparent justification of how trade-offs were made. The research questions are: *What aggregation operators preserve the safety properties of the constituent constraints?* *Under what conditions can incompatible constraint sets be composed without arbitrary loss of stakeholder intent?* *And what are the impossibility results — the constraint-composition analogues of Arrow's theorem — that bound what any such framework can achieve?* This thread addresses the question every real-world deployment eventually faces: ***whose constraints, composed how?***

### 4. Mechanistic Enforcement

The fourth prerequisite is ensuring that constraint satisfaction is enforced where it matters: inside the model's computation, not at the model's output. Current safety practice predominantly enforces constraints through **output-level filtering** - refusal classifiers, content moderation layers, post-hoc rejection — which adversarial inputs can systematically circumvent because the underlying model is unconstrained and only its output is filtered. Mechanistic enforcement asks instead: *which internal circuits implement the satisfaction of a given constraint, and can we verify constraint satisfaction at the level of those circuits?* This thread bridges two communities that have remained largely disconnected: the **mechanistic interpretability** community, which has developed methods for understanding what computations a model performs internally, and the **constraint enforcement** community, which has developed methods for shaping what a model outputs. The synthesis is a research program on *internally-grounded verification*: identifying the circuits responsible for constraint-relevant behavior, characterizing their reliability under distributional and adversarial shift, and certifying the constraint at the substrate level. The central questions are: *What is the right notion of "circuit" for constraint enforcement - features, attention heads, residual-stream subspaces, or something else?* *Under what conditions does mechanistic enforcement provide stronger guarantees than behavioral enforcement?* *And how do we detect when a model has learned to satisfy a constraint by superficial pattern-matching rather than by mechanistic implementation?* This thread ensures the container holds **at the level of the substrate**, not merely at the surface.

### Summary

Through these questions, I aim to move beyond purely statistical reasoning toward generative systems that are **correct by construction, certifiable under bounded error, composable across stakeholders, and mechanistically based** in their internal computation. My long-term ambition is for this program to constitute the foundational substrate on which any superintelligent system - regardless of who builds it or what capability frontier it crosses - must run to be deployable in high-stakes scientific and societal settings. Containment is not a competing capability paradigm; it is the discipline that determines whether any capability paradigm can be safely brought into the world.
