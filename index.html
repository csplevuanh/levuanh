My research develops mathematical methods for reliable and explainable AI. Using tools from applied topology, spectral graph theory, and statistics, I study structural properties that ensure robustness, scalability, and efficiency in machine‚Äëlearning systems.

1. Robustness & Scalability

I study spectral and topological criteria, including Laplacian eigenvalue distributions, diffusion operators, and persistent homology, that quantify how learning algorithms respond to perturbations. These tools provide formal stability guarantees when graphs are edited, communities are split, or the underlying data distribution shifts. I also develop quantization-aware analyses that predict how these guarantees behave in large-scale deployments where numerical precision and hardware constraints can introduce additional errors.

2. Explainability & Diagnostics

To make machine learning models more interpretable, I design structure-aware explanations and post-hoc diagnostic tests that reveal hidden failure modes. Spectral signatures and topological invariants serve as light-weight certifiers that can be embedded directly into training or inference pipelines. These certifiers provide fast, mathematically grounded checks that highlight when a model‚Äôs predictions may be unreliable.

3. Graph Learning

My work provides reliability guarantees for graph filters and graph neural networks (GNNs) operating on non-Euclidean domains, including Œ¥-hyperbolic graphs and other complex topologies. I derive operator-norm and eigenvalue bounds that link small, local graph edits to changes in global predictive behavior. These results give practitioners concrete error bars when deploying GNNs in dynamic or adversarial environments.

4. Efficient Methods

Finally, I develop operator-theoretic and optimization-based objectives that trade off accuracy, computational cost, and reliability. By framing inference and learning as problems in spectral analysis and convex‚Äìnon-convex optimization, I produce practical recipes for scalable training and inference whose behavior can be certified mathematically, even at an industrial scale.
