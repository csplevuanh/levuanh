My research focuses on developing reliable and interpretable AI models. Drawing on techniques from applied topology, graph theory, and statistics, I investigate the structural properties that ensure robustness, scalability, and efficiency in machine learning systems.

1. Robustness & Scalability

I study spectral and topological criteria, including Laplacian eigenvalue distributions, diffusion operators, and persistent homology, that quantify how learning algorithms respond to perturbations. These tools provide formal stability guarantees when graphs are edited, communities are split, or the data distribution shifts. I also develop quantization-aware analyses that predict how these guarantees behave in large-scale deployments where numerical precision and hardware constraints introduce additional errors.

2. Explainability & Diagnostics

To make machine-learning models interpretable, I design structure-aware explanations and post-hoc diagnostic tests that reveal hidden failure modes. Spectral signatures and topological invariants serve as light-weight certifiers that can be embedded into training or inference pipelines, providing fast, theoretically guaranteed checks for when predictions may be unreliable.

3. Graph Learning

I provide reliability guarantees for graph filters and graph neural networks (GNNs) on non-Euclidean domains, including Œ¥-hyperbolic graphs and other complex topologies. I derive operator-norm and eigenvalue bounds that link small, local graph edits to changes in global predictive behavior‚Äîoffering concrete error bars for GNNs in dynamic or adversarial environments.

4. Efficient Methods

I develop operator-theoretic and optimization-based objectives that trade off accuracy, computational cost, and reliability. By framing inference and learning as problems in spectral analysis and convex‚Äìnon-convex optimization, I create practical recipes for scalable training and inference whose behavior can be certified mathematically, even at an industrial scale.
